## Overview

Series 1 of this handbook built Integrated Risk Management on ServiceNow — a mature commercial platform. This page opens Series 2 by asking a different question: can you build a credible GRC capability without a commercial license? The honest answer is yes, for the right organization, with real trade-offs. Open source does not mean free to operate; it means free to modify, self-host, and extend, in exchange for engineering and hosting effort.

This introduction frames the decision. It defines what open-source GRC is, who it fits, and — just as important — who it does not fit, so you can make a sober choice rather than an ideological one.

## When open source makes sense

* **Budget-constrained organizations** — smaller financial institutions, fintech startups, and non-profits that cannot justify a six-figure GRC license but still face real regulatory expectations.
* **Engineering-rich teams** — organizations with the in-house capacity to install, configure, and maintain self-hosted tooling.
* **Transparency requirements** — teams that need to inspect and audit the exact logic behind their risk calculations rather than trust a vendor black box.
* **Learning and evaluation** — practitioners who want to understand the GRC data model deeply before committing to a commercial platform.

## When open source does not make sense

* **No engineering capacity** — if nobody can own patching, backups, and uptime, self-hosted tooling becomes a liability rather than an asset.
* **Scale-heavy vendor risk** — managing thousands of third parties with continuous monitoring is where open-source tooling hits real limits.
* **Regulator-facing reporting at scale** — board-grade dashboards and examination support are significantly harder without a commercial data model.

## Key takeaways

* Open-source GRC is a credible option — not a compromise — for organizations that match its profile.
* The real cost of open source is operational, not licensing: you trade dollars for engineering effort.
* The discipline determines maturity, not the platform; a well-run open-source implementation can reach Level 3 maturity.
* Choose deliberately: this series makes the trade-offs explicit rather than evangelical.
